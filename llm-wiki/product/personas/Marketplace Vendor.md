## Content

### Goals
- Self-manage their slice of the marketplace: create and edit products with variants, browse and fulfill their orders, get paid reliably.
- Understand their performance — reports on sales and inventory to optimize what they list.

### Pain Points
- Clunky day-to-day tooling: anything slower or more confusing than the marketplace tools they already know costs them money.
- Orders tangled with other vendors': they need their own per-vendor orders, with shipping and payment terms they control, to fulfill correctly.
- Limited autonomy: warehouse, channel, and store settings they can't manage themselves mean waiting on the marketplace owner.

### Behavior Patterns
- **Frequency:** Weekly to daily depending on order volume; spiky around fulfillment.
- **Technical level:** Novice–intermediate; thinks in products and orders, not platforms.
- **Decision process:** Joins because the marketplace owner recruits them; stays only if the panel costs them less time than their alternative channel.
- **Current solution:** Selling on incumbent marketplaces (Amazon, Etsy, Allegro) or via email/spreadsheet arrangements with the marketplace owner.

### Key Quote
> "If listing a product takes longer here than on Amazon, I'll just sell on Amazon."

### Product Implications
- The Vendor Panel must hit baseline usability before adding features; vendors compare it to mature marketplace tooling, not to other open-source projects.
- Per-vendor order splitting and reliable payouts are the marketplace foundation — without them, no vendor stays.
- Keep the panel task-oriented and simple (orders, products, payouts, reports); resist exposing the commerce engine's full complexity to vendors.
